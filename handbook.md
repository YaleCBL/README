# Computational Biomechanics Laboratory Handbook

*Based on: [Marsden Lab](https://cbcl.stanford.edu/) Handbook at Stanford University*

## Introduction

Welcome to the Computational Biomechanics Laboratory! This document lays out expectations for people working in my research group. It is an ever-evolving document that has resulted from my own experience and conversations and input from others.

Please read it and discuss with me if anything does not make sense. I'm always happy to talk and clarify. Nothing is unchangeable, but I ask that you abide by the expectations as written here unless we have an explicit conversation in which we agree to some change.

## Personal and Professional Conduct

**Work Ethic:** I expect you to be self-motivated, and the hours worked should never be an issue. The freedom to set your own schedule is one of the many perks of the academic lifestyle. I strongly suggest, however, that you be in the lab in person most days for most of the usual workday to foster interactions with your colleagues. In my experience, there is a minimum number of hours that you have to work to keep momentum going in your research. For those lab members who are jointly advised, you may regularly split time between more than one lab. In addition, it is understood that having the freedom to adjust your schedule can help make academia more welcoming and affordable to those starting families. Weekends are primarily for relaxing and recharging, but keeping the momentum going at work is important, and I expect many trainees will spend some time on the weekend keeping their projects moving forward (e.g. babysitting simulations, editing manuscripts).

**Vacation time and absences:** Please let me know if you will be out of the lab for more than one day and the duration and general reason for that absence (e.g., vacation, conference, etc.). This is so we can plan conversations and meetings appropriately. I expect people to take four weeks of vacation a year (in addition to the two weeks of campus shutdown around Christmas and New Year).

**Behavior in the lab:** The Computational Biomechanics Laboratory and Yale University are wholly committed to maintaining a professional and supportive community. Professionalism encompasses (1) respect for colleagues in every department at every rank, visitors, and vendors; (2) maintaining the highest standards of integrity, working hard, and being proud of your work; and (3) being reliable, honest, and presenting yourself competently and confidently. Support entails (1) academically challenging and bettering each other via constructive criticism, teaching, and collaboration and (2) placing importance on the success of your colleagues, lab, and University. Please remember that you represent Yale University and our lab, and your behavior within and outside the University reflects on us all.

Disagreements will arise, of course, but I expect those to be resolved professionally and courteously. If you are tempted to raise your voice, don't – please explain your position calmly, and if no resolution is forthcoming, walk away. When both parties are calm, return to the conversation, possibly out of the lab. I'd rather engage in any dispute with the individuals concerned and help resolve it (individually or together) than see the culture of the lab become one where arguing and hostility occur. While stimulating academic debates are encouraged, arguing is not, and serious or repeated offenses should be brought to my attention.

Harassment (emotional, verbal, or physical) experienced by anyone in the lab (whether from within or outside our group) is unacceptable and should be reported to me or the relevant university office immediately. Concerns can and should be brought to my attention at any time. Sexual harassment will not be tolerated and will be reported (as mandated by law) through the appropriate channels. Please review [Yale's Title IX policies](https://titleix.yale.edu/).

**Work-life balance and Mental Health:** A critical component of being an effective scientist is learning to care for yourself. Being an undergraduate student, graduate student, postdoc, staff, or faculty member at Yale can sometimes be difficult. During your time in the lab, I expect you to make time for things that help you maintain your general mental and physical health and well-being! This includes exercise, sports, creative arts, cooking, and spending time with friends and family. If you are struggling, please reach out to me so that we can brainstorm ways to help you and identify mental health resources that may be useful to you. Some specific resources that may be useful include [Yale Well](https://yalewell.yale.edu/mental-health/mental-health-counseling-yale-health) and [Yale Health](https://yalehealth.yale.edu/department/mental-health-counseling).

**Academic Integrity:** I cannot stress enough the importance of maintaining academic integrity and sound ethics in all that we do in the lab. This comes in several forms. We must be honest and transparent about all data we generate and the methods we develop. I will do my best to help ensure your work is publishable regardless of whether your results come out "as expected" as long as you are honest and transparent with the rest of our team and me about your findings and methods. Papers should be your own written work, and it is never okay to copy or plagiarize from the writing of others. Especially since we open source our code, you should assume your simulations and post-processing will be checked and re-run by others inside or outside our lab. Cite relevant original work that you incorporated into your process. Remember that if you are funded on a federal grant, I have to sign and personally certify on behalf of the lab regarding the integrity of our processes and the proper use of funds. Thankfully, we have not had prior issues of this nature in the lab, and I don't want there to be a first time!

**Time management:** Effective time management is critical to your success. Some "best practices" that can help with time management are: (1) setting a Sunday night meeting with yourself to plan what you will accomplish that week, (2) setting aside dedicated time for writing (e.g. one hour every morning), (3) blocking off time in your calendar for research in advance (instead of waiting until you have time to do it, which generally doesn't "just happen"), (4) being mindful of balancing your research with the time you spend on coursework, service activities, and other commitments, (5) ask yourself "Is this activity leading to a paper or a fellowship?", (6) you should not give up too easily, nor should you spend hours and hours stuck on one thing without asking for help. Asking for help is often a sign of confidence, not weakness, especially when it helps you move forward more efficiently.

**Values:** A lab environment that brings together various experiences, perspectives, and backgrounds is essential to attracting, engaging, and retaining talented group members, as well as furthering our record of scientific excellence and innovation. Fostering this environment is a central mission of the lab, which takes work and effort. I welcome suggestions from all lab members about ways I can help make the lab a more welcoming environment.

## Research Expectations

**Publications and Productivity:** I recommend targeting three first-author papers (for PhD students or postdocs) before graduation or leaving the lab. You should also aim to be a co-author on three additional publications, generally written by other lab members you collaborate with on other projects. The exact number can change depending on (1) the publication venue or "meatiness" of the paper, (2) how long you stay in the lab, or (3) your career goals (e.g. industry focused trainees may wish to prioritize software skills and "GitHub resume" over publications). My goal is not only for you to graduate but to land a competitive position after you leave. In my experience, a strong body of published work is your best advantage. Academic jobs are hard to get, but the main things you have control over are publications and funding. Targeting 10–15 papers by the end of your postdoc and obtaining pre/postdoc funding (e.g. a fellowship or other award) is one of the most straightforward ways to be competitive.

**Collaborations and Authorship:** I pride myself on our lab's collegial and collaborative culture. Because our work in cardiovascular modeling is highly interdisciplinary, it is necessary (and fun!) to work with others inside and outside the lab. Often, this requires communicating across different disciplines (e.g. engineering and cardiology). You are encouraged to ask questions. Open discussion of any issues that arise is encouraged.

Authorship on joint papers should and will be discussed early, openly, and often. As a paper evolves, sometimes authorship contributions may as well. Always remember that the scientific community is small – maintaining a positive reputation for being a good collaborator will take you far. In general, trainees should be the first authors of papers for which they did most of the work and writing. I will generally be the senior (and corresponding) author on papers written by trainees in the lab. However, I sometimes trade this role with collaborators to share credit and recognition for joint work.

If you are the lead author on a manuscript and you leave the lab before the work is completed, we will do our best to keep you in a lead or prominent authorship position – but often, it will be necessary to do additional simulations, perform revisions, and carry out new analyses that will require substantial effort from another lab member. In these cases, it will be important to ensure that those lab members who carry out this additional work are appropriately credited (and incentivized to help push your paper through to publication!). In my experience, it is best to avoid this situation and submit the paper yourself before or near the time you finish in the lab.

**Individual Meetings:** I meet with everyone in the lab one-on-one for 30 minutes every other week. These meetings serve many purposes, including updating me on your professional planning, future directions, the status of manuscripts, and troubleshooting research. I am always interested in getting to know you as a person. Please come to the meeting prepared with slides (informal is fine) outlining what you accomplished since our previous meeting and plans for what you plan to do next. Keep in mind there are a lot more of you than there are of me, so it sometimes takes me a minute to "code switch" from one project to the next – please be patient if you have to refresh my memory on the current status of your project. I suggest you document notes from the meeting (including the goals that we lay out at the end of the meeting for your next 2 weeks of research) so that at the start of the following meeting, we can go over the previous goals and see how the progress looks. If my door is shut, please knock – I often continue meeting people until the next person is ready to meet.

If I am unavailable during your regularly scheduled meeting, we can skip that week's meeting or schedule another time if you would still like to meet. I will try to give as much notice of any change as possible, but please be understanding if things occasionally slip through the cracks or change unexpectedly. If you will be out of town, please let me know to cancel your meeting so I can give the time slot to someone else.

**Lab meetings:** We hold lab meetings weekly, and attendance is mandatory for all lab members unless you have a direct conflict with a class or you are on travel. We use the following format: first, each person gives a one-minute update in which you tell us what you accomplished the previous week, what you plan to do next, and anything you need from me or the group. Then, we have presentations on research updates from two lab members. These are scheduled on a rotating basis. When I am out of town, we often hold a journal club or a training session instead. During lab meetings, I ask you to keep laptops closed and refrain from checking your phones out of respect for the presenter.

Learning to give clear and well-motivated talks is one of the most important skills you can gain as a graduate student or a postdoc. Being a good speaker helps you as an academic (securing a job, securing funding, teaching, attracting students), in industry (securing a job, pitching ideas, communicating progress, securing funding), and in pretty much any job I can think of. Group meetings are the perfect venue for you to practice and improve on this and to tell your labmates about what you have been working on and get their feedback. It is most useful to present work in progress (rather than polished/finished) so we can discuss your next steps or any areas you are stuck on.

In addition, being an audience member during group meetings allows you to learn about new science in the lab and think about how you would give constructive feedback to help this person's science be as impactful as possible. When not presenting, please participate by asking questions, following up afterward, and giving constructive feedback.

**Attending Seminars:** I expect you to attend scientific seminars during the academic year, aligning with your interests. Choices include:
- [Yale Translational Research Imaging Center](https://medicine.yale.edu/internal-medicine/cardio/research/translational-imaging/). Contact [Ibolya Csecs](ibolya.csecs@yale.edu)
- Cardiovascular Biology Research in Progress. Contact [Sandra Gizzi](sandra.gizzi@yale.edu)
- Myocardial Biology Seminar. Contact [Vascular Biology and Therapeutics Program](yale.vbtprogram@yale.edu)

Going together with other lab members is encouraged so you can discuss.

**Clinical observation:** I strongly encourage you to take advantage of the opportunity to do clinical observation during your time in the lab. This can include watching a surgery or cath lab procedure, going on rounds in the CVICU, and visiting the radiology suite. You should not leave Yale without doing this. There are many opportunities to shadow and observe procedures with our clinical collaborators and partners, but you have to take the initiative and make it happen. Whether or not it directly relates to your research, I can promise you an impactful and meaningful experience.

**Handling patient data:** Because our lab frequently handles patient data in our simulation pipeline, we must adhere to all guidelines set out for the proper handling of Protected Health Information (PHI). PHI is any information in the medical record or designated record set created, used, or disclosed while providing a health care service, such as diagnosis or treatment, that can be used to identify an individual. Everyone in the lab (whether you are directly handling PHI data or not) MUST take the proper CITI training, which covers proper procedures and data handling for human subjects research.

## Expectations for Me

In my role as your advisor, I will:

- [Maintain a supportive lab environment](https://direct.mit.edu/jocn/article-pdf/35/1/44/2061264/jocn_a_01928.pdf).
- Work hard to obtain financial support for the lab so that you can focus on science.
- Help you manage teams and relationships with collaborators and co-workers.
- Provide you with scientific and professional guidance that furthers your progress toward your professional goals; it is your life, not mine.
- Recognize that our goals are usually aligned – your success is my success.
- Treat all equally and with respect.
- Pay all equally using a transparent pay structure such that all with comparable credentials and experience receive the same pay, regardless of their personal situation.
- Be professional at all times and always err on the side of discretion in how I handle all situations and information.
- Respect your time.
- Give credit where credit is due, especially in public presentation of any work done in the lab.
- Not be the "bottleneck" in getting manuscripts submitted.
- Be a life-long mentor to the extent you engage me in that role.

## Computing

**Software Practices:** Our lab strongly supports an open-source software culture. We encourage everyone to use good practices for writing and managing software. Any code you write for your research must be version-controlled and stored on GitHub. This is for your own benefit and the benefit of the lab. Using GitHub applies whether you are doing solver development, SimVascular development, or just writing a script for your own use. It will allow you to easily share what you've done with others and benefit from code developed by others. It will save and track your changes and backup your source code in a reliable manner. Good software practices and GitHub skills will benefit you in all your future positions and are expected of any modern software developer.

In general, you should expect the lab to open-source the code you develop during your time with us. We often wait until after publication to make sure you get proper visibility for the work. This will benefit you in that you will continue to have access to the code after you leave Yale. It will also help to advance the field and benefit us and the community in a variety of ways (e.g. being eligible for software grants, gaining visibility in the field, and having our work used and cited by others).

**Cluster Usage:** Our lab has high-performance computing resources on [Bouchet](https://docs.ycrc.yale.edu/clusters/bouchet/). You can [request an account here](https://research.computing.yale.edu/support/hpc/account-request). Please respect others' needs when requesting compute nodes on the cluster. In general, you should use your local computer to develop and debug code, and clusters only for larger "production" runs for publishable results. Be mindful that these resources are shared with other lab members. Please note that a cluster's scratch space is purged regularly. You should not store important simulations or data on the scratch disk, or you risk significant data loss.

**Backups:** Computers die unexpectedly. You must back up all data on your computer. Install any of the [Yale supported services](https://yale.service-now.com/it?id=service_offering&sys_id=ac684dcd6fbb31007ee2abcf9f3ee4f9) as soon as you get your computer.

## Conferences

**Frequency:** We have a [running document for conference planning](https://docs.google.com/document/u/0/d/e/2PACX-1vQcfep_N_cnQF43CeNJSGk1cPOzL0nnBC7_oXGZbgMS4pnVV89hsmJsa7vcKgTkA2Y8ASn-UaMMGaMl/pub). Generally, you should plan to attend up to two conferences per year, and each abstract / presentation should include new results. Trainees should only attend meetings where they are submitting an abstract. Often, it makes sense to attend meetings with different focuses (e.g. computational science, fluid mechanics, biomechanics, cardiology). Please give me your abstract for editing at least one week before the deadline.

**Preparing your talk:** Unless you have given essentially the same talk before, please plan to practice with whomever from the group can come (sometimes, we dedicate a lab meeting to this if many people are attending the same conference). Likewise, please try to attend a practice talk and give input. It is useful to criticize and then apply those criticisms of others to your own talk. I also recommend inviting someone from outside the lab to the talk to catch the omission of things that we all take as "given," but others don't (e.g., use of lab-specific jargon, system-specific "insider" knowledge, etc.).

I have truly never been anything other than proud to listen to a presentation from someone in the lab. That doesn't mean we can't all improve our presentation skills, and I will provide feedback for both the practice talk and the actual talk. Please provide me with an electronic copy of your talk; these are invaluable summations of your work and complement the more detailed lab meeting presentations.

Including work by others in the lab (or our collaborators) can often strengthen a talk, whether those data were obtained as part of a collaborative effort or just something someone else has obtained. If you do this, please:

1. Talk to the person concerned and get explicit permission to include their (or your "collective") results in your talk.
2. Acknowledge the person's contribution verbally during the talk and in writing on the relevant slide(s); including a photo of the person helps remind you to do this and helps people in the audience seek out the person to learn more about the result.

**Preparing your poster:** Less is more. Many people, myself included, find posters hard to take in because the space for poster sessions is often dimly lit, extremely crowded, noisy, and full of distractions. As a result, they should not be a paper that you reformat to a poster with tons of words and details. Instead, they should be very light, generally in bullet format and with relatively few words. People passing by need to see a short summary to decide if this is something that they want to take the time to look at in detail. Conclusions are also essential. I like to review posters to help provide input and be sure I know what we are saying/showing publicly. Please ask other lab members for templates and examples. Please send me final copies of all posters electronically for archiving.

**Going to conferences:** Meetings can be invaluable for the information gleaned and contacts formed. Attendance at sessions is key, and I strongly recommend taking good notes, as the amount of information flowing in can be overwhelming and impossible to retain. Using the abstract book works well in many cases, but sometimes, an old-fashioned notebook is best. Possibly the most important aspect of conferences is meeting new people. The resulting network will facilitate your research regarding information learned and future job opportunities. To meet people, you may need to get out of your comfort zone! Don't hang out only with folk you already know. Introduce yourself to those you sit next to and ask, "What do you work on?" and be prepared to do likewise.

**How to get reimbursed:** Please follow all guidelines set forth by Yale when making travel arrangements to avoid any issues or delays with reimbursement.

**Managing costs:** Our lab has finite resources that require significant effort to obtain and manage. This includes discretionary funding and grant funds, neither of which are provided by the university directly. Please be mindful of this as you plan your conference travel. Trainees should share rooms at meetings. Be mindful of keeping costs reasonable as you make your plans. That said, we don't expect you to be inconvenienced or waste significant time to save a small amount of money. For example, it's reasonable to get up earlier to take the cheapest possible direct flight, but it's unnecessary to take a flight with two stops just to save $50. I trust that you will use your best judgment when making such decisions.

## Mentorship

Every graduate student is generally paired with a postdoc for additional mentorship. Likewise, each undergraduate researcher is paired with a graduate student or postdoc mentor. If you wish to mentor a student, many opportunities are available, and I consider this experience part of your training. Please discuss this with me before you promise to mentor anyone from outside the lab. If you wish to host a visitor, please also discuss this with me in advance, as this requires resources you may not be aware of.
